Stage 01
01
Eurorack Mixer
Target: clean output, no clipping on mixer meters

Modular signals run hot — roughly ±5V to ±10V — far louder than standard line level. Your job here is to keep the mixer's output clean before the signal hits the converter module. Everything downstream depends on getting this right.

Steps

  1. 1 Play your patch at a typical session volume — the level you'd actually use, not a test tone.
  2. 2 Watch the mixer's output meters while your patch runs. Peaks should be healthy but not hitting the ceiling.
  3. 3 Adjust individual channel levels and the master output so you have headroom to spare.
  4. 4 If your mixer has a master output trim or attenuator, use it to tame the signal before it exits the rack — better to attenuate here than boost later.

Measuring with the Mordax Data

Patch your mixer's stereo output into two inputs on the Mordax Data before it reaches the line out module.

  • In oscilloscope mode, look at the peak voltage. A healthy Eurorack output runs around ±5V peak.
  • If the waveform is flat-topped at the top or bottom, you are clipping — reduce the mixer output immediately.
  • Use VU/meter mode on the Mordax Data for a time-averaged read rather than just instantaneous peaks.
Note The Mordax Data inputs are designed for Eurorack-level signals (±5V to ±10V). Only use it on the Eurorack side of your chain — before the line out module converts the signal to line level. Readings after the converter will be inaccurate.
Stage 02
02
Line Out Module
Target: +4 dBu output / ~1.23V RMS

Your line out converter module steps the Eurorack-level signal down to a standard line level. This is the most critical translation point in your chain — the signal crosses from the modular world into the pro audio world here.

Steps

  1. 1 Check whether your module has a level switch or trim pot. Set it to +4 dBu output if available — this is the better choice for the Zoom L6.
  2. 2 If your module only outputs −10 dBV (consumer level), it will still work — you'll just need slightly more input gain on the Zoom.
  3. 3 Play your patch and listen carefully for any distortion introduced at this stage. If the module is being overdriven, reduce the mixer output from Stage 1.

Measuring the output

  • Use a multimeter set to AC voltage on the module's output jacks. A +4 dBu signal reads approximately 1.23V RMS.
  • The Mordax Data can be used here if the module outputs at Eurorack-compatible voltage, but once converted to true line level the readings will be low.
  • The most practical check is to watch the Zoom L6's input meters in the next stage and work backwards if levels seem off.
Tip If you have a choice between +4 dBu and −10 dBV on your module, always use +4 dBu when feeding the Zoom L6. The higher level means a better signal-to-noise ratio and a more robust signal through the cable run.
Stage 03
03
Zoom LiveTrak L6
Target: peaks at −12 to −6 dBFS, faders at unity

The Zoom LiveTrak L6 receives your stereo line-level signal and routes it to its outputs. Your goal here is to set the input trim so the Zoom's converters are working in their optimal range, then pass the signal through cleanly with faders at unity.

Target meter range
Too quiet (below −20 dBFS) fix upstream
Target zone (−12 to −6 dBFS) ✓ good
Clipping (0 dBFS) reduce gain

Steps

  1. 1 Connect your line out module's stereo output to the stereo line input on the Zoom L6.
  2. 2 Play your patch and watch the input channel meter on the L6.
  3. 3 Adjust the input trim (gain knob) on the stereo channel so peaks land between −12 and −6 dBFS — the green/yellow region on the meter.
  4. 4 Set the channel fader to unity (the 0 or U position). Do not use the fader to compensate for a weak signal — fix it upstream.
  5. 5 Set the master output fader to unity as well and confirm the output meters match what you expect.
Heads up Leave headroom for dynamics. Modular synthesis can produce sudden loud transients that your average level won't predict. Keeping peaks at −12 to −6 dBFS means a sudden spike will reach around −6 dBFS rather than clipping at 0.
Stage 04
04
Apollo Twin Duo X
Target: avg −18 dBFS, peaks no higher than −6 dBFS

The Apollo Twin Duo X is your final recording stage. By the time signal arrives here it should already be well-leveled from upstream. The Apollo's job is to convert the signal accurately — not to add significant gain.

Steps

  1. 1 Connect the Zoom LiveTrak L6's main output to the Apollo Twin Duo X's line inputs.
  2. 2 Open Universal Audio Console on your Mac Studio.
  3. 3 Play your patch and watch the input channel meter in Console.
  4. 4 Set the Apollo's input gain so average program level sits around −18 dBFS, with peaks approaching −6 dBFS on louder moments.
  5. 5 In your DAW, add a meter plugin as an insert on the input track for a detailed read. Free options: MeldaProduction MFAQ, or your DAW's stock meters.

DAW metering tips

  • Place the meter before any processing on the input channel — you want to see the raw signal.
  • Aim for an average RMS level around −18 to −14 dBFS. This is the digital sweet spot for 24-bit recording.
  • Enable true peak mode if your meter supports it — it catches intersample peaks that standard peak metering misses.
Tip The Apollo Twin Duo X has excellent converters — trust them. If you find yourself cranking the Apollo's input gain to compensate for a weak signal, that is a sign something is too quiet upstream. Go back and fix it at Stage 1 or 2.
Calibration workflow

Full chain setup

Run this process at the start of each session, or any time you make changes to your rack. Uses a test tone to take the guesswork out of level setting.

What you need

  • A sustained test tone — a 1kHz sine wave from an oscillator module at a typical session level
  • The Mordax Data in your rack
  • The Zoom LiveTrak L6 powered and connected
  • UA Console open on your Mac Studio
1
Patch your oscillator into the Eurorack mixer at a level typical for your sessions. Set it to a sustained 1kHz sine tone.
2
Patch the mixer output into the Mordax Data. In oscilloscope mode, confirm a clean sine wave with peaks around ±5V. No flat-topping.
3
Patch the mixer output into your line out module. Adjust the module's trim so it outputs +4 dBu if possible (verify with a multimeter: ~1.23V RMS).
4
Watch the Zoom LiveTrak L6's input meter. Adjust the L6's input trim until the tone reads around −12 dBFS. Set channel and master faders to unity.
5
Open UA Console and confirm the signal arriving at the Apollo is around −18 to −12 dBFS for a sustained tone.
6
Open your DAW and confirm the input track meter matches Console. Place a meter plugin before any processing inserts.
7
Replace the test tone with your actual patch. Play a typical passage and confirm nothing clips at any stage. You're done.
Remember Gain staging is not a one-time setup. Revisit it any time you significantly change your patch, add new modules, or change routing through your rack. A patch that was well-leveled with one configuration may clip with another.
Troubleshooting

Common problems

If something sounds off, start here before adjusting anything downstream.

Signal clipping on the Zoom L6 meters
Reduce the line out module's trim, or lower the Eurorack mixer's output level. Do not reduce the L6's input trim as a first resort — fix it upstream.
Signal too quiet at the Apollo
Increase the level at Stage 1 (mixer output) or Stage 2 (line out module trim). Do not over-boost the Apollo's input gain to compensate.
Flat-topped waveform on Mordax Data
You are clipping in the Eurorack. Reduce channel levels or the mixer's master output before the signal leaves the rack.
Noise or hiss audible in the signal
Check that the line out module is set to +4 dBu (not −10 dBV). Also confirm the Zoom L6 input trim is not set too high — excessive input gain adds noise.
Levels inconsistent between sessions
Run the calibration workflow with a test tone at the start of each session to confirm levels have not drifted between patch changes.
